Overview

Course Explorer

Goal & Usage

Course Explorer is not just another course listing website. Instead, it focuses on providing data on courses that might influence a student's choice of academic schedule, such as required time committment and whether it fulfills the WRIT requirement, etc.

It allows students to sort and filter on attributes they like:
If a student prefers courses with an in-person component, the option to filter on Instuction Mode could become helpful;
If a student is looking for a course with lighter workload, sorting from low to high on Avg. Time Committment is a great way to start.

In other words, the types of information it shows is much closer to that of a course review website, such as The Critical Review at Brown, than a course registrar. While not currently implemented, the application can also be extended so that the information is directly scraped from The Critical Review instead.

Usability Principles

Learnability:

The app utilizes lots of affodances to give clues to new users as to how to navigate the application. For example, most of the buttons have a vivid shade of color, which sets it apart from the "non-interactable" text.

The navigation bar with buttons for filters & sorting orders also has a slight shade of grey as its background color that makes it stand out from the rest of the app. It also happens to be the place where most user interaction takes place, thus making it easier to navigate even for first-time users, as most of the buttons/actions are all grouped together in one area.

Efficiency & Memorability:

The application has a intuitive layout, with a view of the courses & the conditions to be applied to the view (e.g. filters) on one side, and the course cart on the other. When a user adds a course to their cart, they can immediately see it appear on the right, which offers a quick confirmation as a reponse to their action.

It also has great memorability both due to the ease of completing desired tasks on the interface, as well as the fact that since most people are already familiar to the idea of picking an item and placing it into a cart (whether it is a physical cart or an online shopping cart), there's not much of a problem with trying to re-learn how to navigate the interface even if they haven't used it in a long time.
